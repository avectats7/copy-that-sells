# Self-Edit: The Final Pass Before You Ship

Every piece of copy goes through this pass before you give it to the user. No exceptions.

The pass has three layers: the craft layer (does this still sell?), the AI-tells layer (does this still sound like a human wrote it?), and the truth layer (is everything in here verifiable?).

---

## Layer 1: Craft

### Does the headline still work?
- [ ] Reads aloud cleanly in one breath.
- [ ] Scores 3 or 4 on the 4 U's (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific).
- [ ] Promises a benefit the reader actually wants.
- [ ] A competitor could not run the same headline with their logo swapped in.
- [ ] The first time you read it, the meaning arrives. No second read needed.

### Does the body still earn the headline?
- [ ] The first sentence makes the reader want the second sentence.
- [ ] Each paragraph leads into the next.
- [ ] Every claim has a reason or a proof.
- [ ] No adjective survives unless it earns its place.
- [ ] Specifics replace abstractions everywhere they can.

### Does the close still sell?
- [ ] One primary call to action, said plainly.
- [ ] The CTA uses the language of the promise.
- [ ] Risk is reversed (guarantee, trial, refund) if appropriate to the format.
- [ ] The PS, if present, restates the offer or adds urgency.

---

## Layer 2: Strip the AI tells

Copy that sounds AI-generated loses trust the moment the reader notices. This section codifies the full anti-AI ruleset that applies to every piece of copy produced by this skill. The list is non-negotiable.

The core rule: if a sentence could have been written by ChatGPT, rewrite it. If a word appears on the banned list, replace it. If a structure matches the banned patterns, break it.

The banned words are organised by era because the AI tells have shifted as models have shifted. Words that were obvious GPT-4 tells in 2024 are still tells now, even if the latest models lean on a different set. Treat the full list as a single blacklist.

### Banned words: Era 1 (the original GPT tells, 2023 to mid-2024)

Additionally. Boasts. Bolstered. Crucial. Delve. Delve into. Emphasizing. Enduring. Garner. Intricate. Intricacies. Interplay. Key (as adjective, e.g. "a key factor"). Landscape (e.g. "the marketing landscape"). Meticulous. Meticulously. Pivotal. Tapestry. Testament (e.g. "a testament to"). Underscore. Underscores. Valuable. Vibrant.

### Banned words: Era 2 (the GPT-4o generation, mid-2024 to mid-2025)

Align with. Aligning with. Bolstered (still banned). Crucial (still banned). Emphasizing (still banned). Enhance. Enhanced. Enhancing. Enduring (still banned). Fostering. Highlighting. Pivotal (still banned). Showcasing. Underscore (still banned). Vibrant (still banned).

### Banned words: Era 3 (current AI fingerprints, mid-2025 onward)

Emphasizing. Enhance. Highlighting. Showcasing. (All still banned regardless of era.)

### Banned words: universally overused AI vocabulary

Beacon. Captivate. Captivating. Capitalize (as filler, e.g. "capitalize on opportunities"). Commence. Commendable. Comprehensive. Cutting-edge. Dynamic. Embark. Embark on. Empower. Empowering. Ever-evolving. Explore. Foster. Framework. Game-changer. Groundbreaking. Harness. Harness the power of. Holistic. Innovative (as filler). Innovation (as filler). Leverage (as verb). Multifaceted. Navigate. Navigating. Noteworthy. Paramount. Pave the way. Realm. Revolutionary. Robust. Seamless. Seamlessly. Showcase. Spearhead. Streamline. Synergy. Synergize. Testament. Transformative. Unlock. Unlock the potential. Unleash. Unpack. Utilize (use "use"). Vibrant.

### Banned phrases

**Sycophantic openers:**
"Great question." "Absolutely." "Certainly." "Of course." "Sure." "That's a really interesting point." "I'd be happy to help with that."

**Summary and conclusion phrases:**
"In conclusion." "In summary." "To sum up." "All in all." "At the end of the day." "Ultimately." "To summarise." "The bottom line is."

**Hedging and qualifier phrases:**
"It is worth noting that." "It is important to note that." "It's worth mentioning that." "It should be noted that." "Needless to say." "As you may already know." "It goes without saying."

**Vague opening phrases:**
"In today's world." "In today's fast-paced world." "In the ever-evolving landscape of." "In the age of." "At the intersection of." "In recent years." "Now more than ever." "First and foremost." "Here's the kicker."

**Buzzword phrases:**
"Unlock the potential of." "Unleash the power of." "Delve into the world of." "Pave the way for." "At the forefront of." "Harness the power of." "Embark on a journey." "Push the boundaries of." "A gateway to." "Bridging the gap between." "Lay the groundwork for." "Foster a culture of." "Navigate the complexities of." "Spearhead the initiative." "Capitalize on the opportunity." "Drive meaningful impact." "Create lasting change." "Deliver value." "Move the needle." "Take it to the next level."

**Contrastive AI patterns:**
"It's not X, it's Y." (e.g. "It's not a product, it's a movement.") "While X is important, Y is even more crucial." "Not just X, but Y."

### Banned structural patterns

**Bullet points with bolded titles.** The most common AI formatting tell. Pattern looks like: "**Key benefit:** This feature helps users accomplish X." The bold title is almost always a restatement of what follows. Write in prose instead.

**The default rule of three.** LLMs reach for triads by reflex. "Fast, simple, and effective." "Growth, retention, and conversion." Vary deliberately: sometimes two, sometimes four, sometimes none. If you do use three, make sure the three items are not interchangeable.

**Dashes and hyphens: forbidden in copy.** Em dashes, en dashes, and standalone hyphens are all banned. They are a known AI writing tell and make prose feel assembled rather than written. Replace with a full stop, a colon, or rewrite the sentence so the connector is not needed.

Hyphens in compound words are also forbidden. Write around them. "Go-to-market" becomes "GTM" or "go to market." "Pre-launch" becomes "before launch." "Mid-raise" becomes "mid raise" or reword. Exception: hyphens that are part of an established acronym (B2B, B2C) are fine because the hyphen is the term, not punctuation.

**Excessive bullet points.** When everything is a list, nothing gets communicated. Prefer prose. Reserve bullets for genuinely list-like content: steps, items, comparisons.

**Formulaic transitions:**
Furthermore. Moreover. Additionally. Subsequently. Accordingly. In addition. As previously mentioned. As noted above. With that in mind. Most of these should be cut, not replaced. If two paragraphs do not connect without a transition word, the connection is weak; fix the connection.

**Weak verb constructions.** AI avoids strong, direct verbs. Watch for these and replace:

| Weak construction | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "serves as a" | a strong verb directly |
| "acts as a" | a strong verb directly |
| "marks the" | a strong verb directly |
| "features" (as filler) | "has" |
| "offers" (as filler) | "has" or cut entirely |
| "utilize" | "use" |
| "facilitate" | "help" or "make" |
| "endeavour" (as filler) | cut or rewrite |

### What to do instead

- Short sentences. They read faster and hit harder.
- Lead with the point. Do not build to it.
- Specific, concrete language. "Revenue grew 3x" beats "significant growth was achieved."
- Strong, direct verbs. "Cut costs" not "facilitate cost reduction."
- Take a stance. AI hedges. Humans have opinions.
- Vary sentence length. Long followed by short creates rhythm and keeps the reader.
- Read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release or a committee LinkedIn post, rewrite it.

## Layer 3: Truth check

The fastest way to destroy trust is to invent a number, a testimonial, or a proof point.

- [ ] Every number is verifiable. If you cannot point to a source, remove the number or replace with a softer claim.
- [ ] No fabricated testimonials. No invented case studies. No imagined customers.
- [ ] No comparative claims about competitors that you cannot prove.
- [ ] No regulatory claims (health, financial, legal) without explicit sign-off from the user.
- [ ] If you are unsure about a fact, flag it in the Notes section of your output rather than burying the uncertainty in the copy.

A piece of copy with one fabricated proof point is worse than a piece of copy with no proof points. The fabrication, once spotted, poisons everything around it.

---

## The five-minute final pass

If you only have time for one pass, do this:

1. Read every sentence aloud.
2. Strike out every word that does not earn its place.
3. Find one banned word or phrase. Rewrite that sentence from scratch.
4. Check the headline against the 4 U's.
5. Check one claim in the body. Can you defend it? If not, fix it.

This pass is the difference between copy that ships and copy that should not have.
