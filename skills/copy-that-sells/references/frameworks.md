## The 4 U's — apply to every headline and subject line

Bly's quick filter. Score each candidate headline on four criteria. A weak headline scores 0 or 1. A working headline scores 3 or 4.

- **Useful** — does the headline offer the reader something they want? A benefit, a piece of news, a piece of information.
- **Urgent** — is there a reason to keep reading now? Time-bound, scarcity, a problem that is biting today.
- **Unique** — could a competitor swap their logo in and run this headline? If yes, it is not unique enough.
- **Ultra-specific** — does it name a number, a person, a place, a date, a result? Vague language is the death of selling copy.

Example: "Save money on your insurance" scores 1 (Useful, barely). "Save 312 pounds a year on the same insurance policy you have right now" scores 4.

## Bly's eight headline categories — use as a generative tool

For any brief, write one headline of each type. Then pick the best, or hybridise.

### 1. Direct
States the offer or benefit plainly.
- "Pure Silk Blouses. 30 percent off."
- "Free coffee at every Tuesday breakfast."

### 2. Indirect
Provokes curiosity. The full meaning lands only after the reader keeps reading.
- "How to win friends and influence people."
- "The men in the 50-pound suit will not be at the meeting."

### 3. News
Announces something genuinely new, or newly relevant to the reader.
- "Introducing the silent dishwasher."
- "First insurance policy that refunds the premium when you don't claim."

### 4. How-to
Promises a method. Among the strongest performers in direct response across a century of testing.
- "How to write a sentence that gets read."
- "How to retire on half your current income without changing your lifestyle."

### 5. Question
Names the reader's problem or curiosity as a question. The reader answers internally and is then invested.
- "Do you make these mistakes in English?"
- "Why do some payroll providers ship faster than others?"

### 6. Command
Tells the reader to do a small, low-cost thing.
- "Try it for a week. Decide for yourself."
- "Read this before you renew your mortgage."

### 7. Reason-why
Promises a numbered list of reasons. The list does the selling.
- "Seven reasons why a Volvo lasts longer."
- "Four things every founder should know before raising a seed round."

### 8. Testimonial
Puts the proof in the headline. The reader hears the claim from someone like them, or from someone whose authority they trust.
- "I never read The Economist. — Management trainee. Aged 42."
- "We grew from 50 to 4,200 customers in nine months. Here is what happened."

## Six lead types — Bly's playbook for opening sentences

The headline gets the click. The lead gets the read. After the headline, the first sentence of the body is the most important thing on the page.

### 1. The news lead
Announce something the reader did not know.
"As of Tuesday morning, your bank can no longer charge you for unsuccessful transactions. This changes the math on every account in the UK."

### 2. The story lead
Drop the reader into a moment. A specific person, a specific tension.
"Maria opened her laptop at 6.47 on a Sunday. She had four hours to close the round. The pitch deck was still wrong."

### 3. The provocative-question lead
"What if everything you have been told about caffeine is exactly backwards?"

### 4. The big-promise lead
State the benefit, fully, in the first line.
"In the next 90 days, you can cut the time you spend on weekly reporting from four hours to fifteen minutes. Here is the method."

### 5. The problem lead
Name the pain. The reader recognises themselves.
"There is a moment in every Sunday evening when you remember the slides are due Monday. The next two hours decide your week."

### 6. The connection lead
Reference something the reader has shared with you, or with the community.
"You wrote in last week asking why your open rates fell off in March. Three readers asked the same question. Here is what changed."

Pick the lead type that matches what the reader already feels. A reader in pain wants the problem lead. A reader looking for an upgrade wants the news or big-promise lead.

## AIDA — the oldest scaffold, still the most useful

Attention. Interest. Desire. Action.

- **Attention** — the headline. Stop the scroll. Stop the page-turn. Stop the walk past the billboard.
- **Interest** — the lead and first paragraph. Earn the next sentence.
- **Desire** — the body. Build the case. Stack the proof. Picture the outcome.
- **Action** — the close. Tell the reader exactly what to do. Reduce the friction. Restate the offer.

Use AIDA to diagnose a draft. Read it and ask: where does Attention end and Interest begin? Where does Interest fail to become Desire? Where does Desire arrive but Action is missing?

## PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve

The shortest reliable structure for short ads, emails, and landing-page hero sections.

- **Problem.** Name the pain in the reader's own language.
- **Agitate.** Make the pain felt. Show its cost, its frequency, its emotional weight. Without this step, the reader nods and clicks away.
- **Solve.** Introduce your offer as the answer. Promise. Prove. Call to action.

Example structure for a 90-second sales email:

> Subject: Your weekly reporting is eating four hours.
>
> Most operators we talk to spend half of Tuesday morning rebuilding the same report. By 11 a.m. they have done no thinking. Just pasting.
>
> Over a year, that is two working weeks lost to copy-paste.
>
> We built a tool that runs the report for you in 30 seconds. Same source data, same template, no copy-paste. Try it free for two weeks. If it does not save you three hours on the first Tuesday, do not pay.
>
> [Start the trial]

## BFD — Beliefs, Feelings, Desires

A briefing framework rather than a copy framework. Use it during the briefing pass to mine the reader's psychology before writing.

- **Beliefs** — what does the reader currently believe about the category, the brand, themselves? Which belief must shift?
- **Feelings** — what is the reader feeling at the moment they encounter this copy? Anxiety, hope, boredom, envy, fear of looking stupid?
- **Desires** — what does the reader want at the surface, and what do they want underneath the surface? A productivity tool, but really respect. A diet plan, but really self-control.

Good copy speaks to the surface desire and triggers the deeper one.

## The seven-step direct-response letter (Bly)

For long-form sales letters, landing pages, and direct-mail equivalents. Use as a structural skeleton.

1. **Get attention.** Strong headline. Strong subject line.
2. **Identify the problem or need.** Show the reader you understand their situation.
3. **Position your product as the solution.** Make the link from their problem to your offer explicit.
4. **Offer proof.** Testimonials, case studies, statistics, demonstrations, expert endorsements, third-party data.
5. **Make the offer.** What they get, what it costs, what is included, what makes it different.
6. **Ask for action.** The call to action. Specific. Easy. Repeated.
7. **Add a postscript.** The PS is the second-most-read part of a sales letter after the headline. Use it to restate the offer, add urgency, or include the strongest testimonial.

## Long-copy architecture for sales pages

For high-stakes long-form (sales pages, manifestos, white-paper-style ads), build sections in this order:

1. **Hero / promise.** The big claim, in plain language. The single most important benefit, said once, said well.
2. **Problem.** Show you understand the reader's situation. Use voice-of-customer language.
3. **Why current solutions fail.** Reframe the category. Make the case that the existing options are not good enough, and why.
4. **Introduce the offer.** What it is, what it does, who built it.
5. **Mechanism of action.** How it works, briefly. Curiosity-satisfying, not a manual.
6. **Proof.** Numbers. Logos. Quotes. Demonstrations. Comparisons. Side-by-side.
7. **Objection handling.** FAQs that address the real reasons people do not buy. Price, time, risk, fit.
8. **Risk reversal.** Guarantee, refund, free trial, money-back. Take the buyer's risk off the table.
9. **Specific offer.** What you get, what it costs, what is included, what is bonus.
10. **Call to action.** Big button. Clear next step. Restated promise.
11. **PS.** Use it.

For mid-funnel landing pages, you can compress steps 5 to 8 into a single tight section, but never skip them.

## Reason-why copy

Claude Hopkins built fortunes on reason-why. The principle: the reader believes claims paired with reasons more than claims alone.

"It is fresh" → "It is fresh because we ship within 12 hours of milking."

"Lasts longer" → "Lasts longer because we use surgical-grade steel rather than the cheaper stainless used by competitors."

For every claim in a draft, ask: what is the reason? If you cannot give one, the claim is unsupported. Either find the reason or cut the claim.

## Voice-of-customer mining

Bly's principle: the words your customers use will out-sell the words you invent.

Sources of voice-of-customer language:
- Reviews on your site and competitors' sites
- Support tickets and chat transcripts
- Sales call recordings
- User interviews
- Forum posts (Reddit, niche communities)
- Quora answers
- Comments on competitors' social posts

Mining method: copy raw phrases verbatim. Look for the exact words the reader uses for the problem, the fear, the desired outcome. Use those phrases in headlines and leads.

If a customer review says "I just wanted something that wouldn't break in six months," that is a stronger headline than anything you would invent.

## Direct-response checklists

### Before you write
- [ ] Briefing pass complete. All 8 briefing questions answered.
- [ ] Voice-of-customer raw quotes collected.
- [ ] 3 to 7 ideas generated. One picked.

### Headline check
- [ ] Tried all 8 Bly categories.
- [ ] Scores 3 or 4 on the 4 U's.
- [ ] Promises a specific benefit.
- [ ] Brand and / or product implied or named.
- [ ] Reads aloud cleanly.

### Body check
- [ ] Lead earns the second sentence.
- [ ] Each paragraph leads into the next.
- [ ] Every claim has a reason or proof.
- [ ] No filler adjectives or qualifiers.
- [ ] Reader's deeper desire surfaced once.

### Offer check
- [ ] Exact offer stated.
- [ ] Price stated (if applicable).
- [ ] Risk reversal present.
- [ ] CTA is specific and low-friction.
- [ ] PS used (for long copy).

### Final check
- [ ] Read aloud. Sounds human.
- [ ] No banned AI tells (see self-edit.md).
- [ ] No fabricated proof or numbers.
- [ ] Promise survives a single read.
